01 — Executive Summary

Four platforms. One question: whose UX wins?

This study evaluates the user experience of four major streaming platforms — Netflix, Hulu, Tubi, and Peacock — through a combination of moderated usability testing, heuristic evaluation, and post-session surveys. Participants ranged in age from 18 to 62 and included both frequent and occasional streamers.

Netflix leads in overall experience coherence and discoverability, though its aggressive autoplay and paywall complexity frustrate users. Hulu impresses with live TV integration but struggles with ad-tier UX. Tubi, as the only fully free platform, delivers a surprisingly competitive experience undercut by ad frequency. Peacock shows promise but reveals inconsistent information architecture that leaves users lost.

02 — Methodology

How we conducted this research

Our mixed-methods approach combined qualitative observation with quantitative scoring to produce a well-rounded evaluation of each platform's user experience. All sessions were conducted remotely via screen-share with think-aloud protocols.

01

Usability Testing

12 moderated sessions (3 per platform), each 45–60 minutes. Participants completed five standardized tasks: signing up, finding a specific title, browsing a genre, adjusting settings, and cancelling/modifying a subscription.

02

Heuristic Evaluation

Three trained UX evaluators independently assessed each platform against Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics, adapted for video streaming contexts. Scores were averaged and discrepancies resolved by consensus.

03

Post-Session Survey

System Usability Scale (SUS) questionnaire administered after each session, supplemented by NPS-style likelihood-to-recommend questions and open-ended satisfaction prompts.

Research Limitations

All sessions reflected a US-based participant pool, skewing the results toward English-language content preferences. International catalog differences, which are significant on Netflix and Hulu, were not evaluated. Mobile-only testing was excluded from this report.

06 — Key Findings

What users actually said

These representative quotes emerged from think-aloud sessions. They capture the emotional texture of each experience far better than scores alone.

Netflix
"I know I want to watch something, but I feel like the algorithm just shows me what it wants me to watch, not what I actually want."

— Participant 4, 31 · reflects the paradox of a high-powered recommendation engine that can feel prescriptive rather than helpful.

Netflix's recommendation rows dominate the entire home screen, which users consistently interpreted as both its greatest strength and most significant frustration. 7 of 12 participants mentioned that "I don't know where to look" when asked to find something outside their usual genre.

Hulu
"The ads break right at the cliffhanger. Every single time. I don't know if that's intentional, but it's infuriating."

— Participant 8, 27 · highlights ad placement as Hulu's most viscerally felt pain point.

Hulu's ad-supported tier generated the most emotional responses of any platform. Users on the no-ads tier rated it 8.1 overall — higher than Netflix. Users on the ad tier rated it 5.9. The experience gap between tiers is the widest of any platform in this study.

Tubi
"I honestly thought it would feel cheap, but it's cleaner than Peacock. I just wish there was more stuff on it."

— Participant 11, 22 · captures Tubi's underdog appeal and its primary competitive disadvantage.

Tubi consistently exceeded user expectations on design quality, earning a mean SUS score of 72 — above industry average for ad-supported platforms. The content library depth was cited as the primary adoption barrier by 9 of 12 participants who had tried it.

Peacock
"I clicked on it, and then it said I needed to upgrade. But I thought I already had the premium? I gave up."

— Participant 3, 54 · illustrates the paywall transparency issue that affected 10 of 12 Peacock sessions.

Peacock's tier gating — where content labeled "free" requires an account, and content labeled "premium" may require Premium Plus — caused the most task failures in the study. 8 participants abandoned the subscription task entirely on Peacock, versus 2 on Netflix.

08 — Recommendations

What each platform should fix first

01
High Priority · Netflix

Reduce Autoplay Aggression

Autoplay previews on hover caused disorientation for 8 of 12 participants. Implement a clear, persistent "pause previews" toggle accessible from the home screen — not buried in account settings. Test a 1.5-second delay before video initiates.

02
High Priority · Hulu

Redesign the Plan Selection Flow

The onboarding plan selection screen exposes four tiers with overlapping features and confusing price anchoring. Consolidate to a two-step model: "With Ads" vs "No Ads," then surface Live TV as a clear add-on. Reduce cognitive load at the point of highest drop-off.

02
High Priority · Peacock

Fix Tier Transparency at the Content Level

Every content card must clearly display which tier is required before a user clicks. The current pattern — where users discover a paywall only after tapping "Watch" — is a primary driver of task abandonment and negative perception. A small badge ("Premium+") on content cards would resolve 80% of reported confusion.

03
Medium Priority · Tubi

Introduce Lightweight Profile Support

Tubi is the only platform without multi-profile support. 6 of 12 participants who share a TV expressed frustration at mixed "Continue Watching" queues. Even a basic two-profile system with separate watch histories would significantly improve household usability and retention.

04
Medium Priority · All Platforms

Standardize Genre Taxonomy

Each platform uses a different genre vocabulary ("Thriller" vs. "Suspense" vs. "Mystery") with no cross-platform consistency. Users who switch between services experience disorientation. Adopting genre tags aligned with IMDB or TMDB conventions would reduce search friction and improve findability.

05
Medium Priority · Hulu + Peacock

Smarter Ad Break Placement

Ad-supported tiers on both platforms insert mid-roll ads at algorithmically chosen intervals, often landing at narrative peaks. Implement scene-boundary detection (available via most video encoding pipelines) to ensure breaks occur at natural pauses — reducing the visceral negative response from participants in this study.

09 — Conclusion

The streaming UX gap is real — and growing

Netflix's continued investment in interface refinement has created a compounding advantage that smaller platforms struggle to match. However, this study reveals that user loyalty is more fragile than platform market share suggests: participants switched platforms readily when content drove them there, and their primary frustrations were consistent regardless of brand preference.

The three universal pain points across all platforms are: ad interruptions that disrupt narrative immersion, confusion about what content is available at which price tier, and discoverability failures outside the algorithmic row format. Any platform that solves even two of these three will disproportionately capture users who are currently satisfied but not loyal.

Tubi's surprising performance in this study is the most actionable signal for incumbents: a platform with no subscription revenue, a lighter content library, and minimal brand marketing still managed to satisfy users at a competitive level — because its interface stayed out of their way. The lesson is less about features and more about restraint.
